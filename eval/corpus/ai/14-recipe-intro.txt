# Braised Short Ribs with Pomegranate and Walnuts

There's a category of dishes that live in the background of memory—the meal your parent or grandparent made that you ate dozens of times but never quite replicated. For me, that dish is my grandmother's braised short ribs. I remembered them as impossibly tender, deeply savory, with a sweet edge that came from dried fruit she never quite named. Twenty years later, I finally got the recipe and adapted it with pomegranate molasses (which I suspect is what she was using).

The magic of braised short ribs is patience. There's no shortcut. You sear the meat to build depth, you braise it low and slow for hours, and the collagen converts to gelatin, the fat renders into the sauce, and what started as a tough cut becomes something you can eat with a wooden spoon. It's not complicated, but it requires time and genuine heat.

This version balances savory with sweet—pomegranate molasses brings tang and acidity, walnuts add texture and bitterness, and the braising liquid becomes a silky sauce that sticks to the meat. It's the kind of dish you make when you're not in a hurry, when you have an oven working for hours while you do other things, and when you want to feed people something they'll remember.

## Ingredients

**For the short ribs:**
- 3-4 lbs beef short ribs (about 4-6 ribs, bone-in)
- Salt and pepper to taste
- 3 tablespoons olive oil
- 1 large yellow onion, rough chop
- 4 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 carrots, rough chop
- 2 tablespoons tomato paste
- 1.5 cups beef or chicken stock (or water)
- 1/2 cup pomegranate molasses
- 2 tablespoons honey
- 2 teaspoons ground cumin
- 1 teaspoon cinnamon
- 1/2 teaspoon cloves
- 2 bay leaves
- 3/4 cup walnuts, coarsely chopped
- Pomegranate seeds for garnish (optional)
- Fresh cilantro or parsley for garnish

## Method

**Prep (15 minutes):** Pat short ribs dry and season generously with salt and pepper on all sides. This is critical—wet meat won't brown. If your ribs are very large, you can cut them in half between bones, but ideally you leave them whole.

**Sear the meat (10 minutes):** Heat a large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add olive oil and let it get shimmering. Working in batches if needed, sear the short ribs on all sides until deeply browned—about 2-3 minutes per side. You want a dark crust. Don't crowd the pan. Remove ribs to a plate.

**Build the aromatics (5 minutes):** In the same pot, add onion and carrot. Cook for 3-4 minutes until starting to color. Add garlic and cook another minute. Stir in tomato paste and let it caramelize on the bottom of the pot for a minute—this adds depth.

**Deglaze (2 minutes):** Pour in a splash of stock and scrape the bottom of the pot to dislodge the browned bits. This is where most of the flavor lives.

**Braise (3 hours):** Return the short ribs to the pot. Add stock, pomegranate molasses, honey, cumin, cinnamon, cloves, and bay leaves. The liquid should come about halfway up the ribs. If you need more, add water or stock. Bring to a bare simmer on the stovetop, then cover and transfer to a 325°F oven.

Braise for 2.5 to 3 hours, stirring occasionally. The meat should be falling-apart tender—if you pierce it with a fork, there should be zero resistance.

**Toast the walnuts (5 minutes):** While the ribs braise, roughly chop walnuts and toast them in a dry skillet over medium heat for 3-4 minutes, stirring frequently. This brings out the bitterness and richness.

**Finish (10 minutes):** Remove the ribs from the oven. Skim excess fat from the surface of the braising liquid if it bothers you (I leave some—it carries flavor). Taste the sauce and adjust seasoning. It should taste savory, slightly sweet, with depth from the spices.

If the sauce is thin and watery, transfer the ribs to a serving plate and simmer the sauce on the stovetop for 5-10 minutes to reduce and concentrate it.

## To Serve

Plate a short rib in a shallow bowl. Ladle sauce around it. Garnish with toasted walnuts, pomegranate seeds if using them, and fresh cilantro or parsley. Serve over rice, polenta, or with good bread to soak up the sauce.

This dish is even better the next day—the flavors marry and deepen. Reheat gently in a 300°F oven for 20 minutes. It freezes beautifully for up to three months.

## Notes

- The pomegranate molasses is essential. It's available at Middle Eastern grocers or online. No good substitute—pomegranate juice will work but won't have the same depth.
- This recipe is forgiving. The braising time can stretch to 3.5 hours if your ribs are very large. The worst thing that can happen is they get more tender.
- Red wine is traditional in braising. I skip it here because the pomegranate plays that role, but you can substitute half the stock for wine if you prefer.
- Make extra sauce. You'll want it with bread.

The first time I made this from memory, I forgot the pomegranate molasses halfway through and had to improvise with pomegranate juice and vinegar. It was fine, but it wasn't the dish I remembered. Since then, it's become a standard in my kitchen—the kind of thing I make when I want to feel like someone who knows what they're doing.
