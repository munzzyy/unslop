Building in public is terrifying. Also the best decision I've made this year.

Three months ago, I shipped the first version of a tool I'd been sitting on for two years. It was rough. Incomplete. Definitely not "LinkedIn launch" ready. I almost waited another six months.

Then I realized: waiting for perfect is just procrastination dressed up in a professional sweater.

I posted it on a Thursday. Expected crickets. Instead, 200+ people signed up in the first week. Most feedback was on *exactly* the stuff I knew was broken. Some users found use cases I'd never considered. Two people are now actively collaborating on features.

Here's what changed my mind: shipping a 70% solution beats shipping 0%. Real users catch things you can't catch alone—not because they're smarter, but because they use the product differently. The learning velocity is incomparable.

Three principles that made this work:

**1. Show your work, not your polish.** I didn't wait for a slick landing page or a perfect brand story. I just explained what the tool does and why I built it. Honest beats impressive.

**2. Ask for specific feedback.** "What do you think?" gets fluff. "Does the import workflow make sense?" gets actionable answers. I made a short survey and got 40 responses in two days.

**3. Commit to iteration, not perfection.** I shipped a v1.1 with top feedback three weeks later. People who tried it early now feel invested—they see their suggestions land. That loyalty is hard to manufacture.

The scariest part was hitting publish. The second scariest part was reading the first piece of critical feedback. But both of those things made the tool better.

If you're sitting on something—a project, a side hustle, a different direction—ask yourself: what am I actually waiting for? Usually it's not more time or better features. It's permission. You don't need it.

Ship it. Let people break it. Fix it together.

That's how things that matter get built.
